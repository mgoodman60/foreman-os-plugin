# Building Types: Rendering Prompt Adjustment Guide

This guide specifies how rendering prompts should be modified based on building type. Each type has distinct visual characteristics, material expressions, and compositional strategies that must be reflected in AI-generated renderings.

## PEMB / Pre-Engineered Metal Building

**Key Visual Characteristics:**
- Crisp, clearly articulated panel lines running horizontally (wall) or along purlins (roof)
- Standing seam roof with pronounced parallel ridges and seam lines
- Large clear-span interior volumes evident in the form (wide spans, minimal interior columns)
- Linear fastener patterns visible along panel edges (typically every 12-16 inches)
- Consistent ribbing or fluting pattern on corrugated or textured panels
- Gable or shallow-slope roofline expressing the rigid frame structure
- Industrial character balanced with the building's use (healthcare, office, etc.)

**Material Tendencies:**
- Wall panels: typically galvanized steel or painted steel, often light gray, silver, or tan
- Roof: standing seam metal, usually light reflective color (white, silver, light gray, or charcoal)
- Trim: painted steel, aluminum storefronts at entry, possible masonry or curtain wall infill
- Base: may have masonry or metal wainscot to ground level

**Scale Cues:**
- Clear delineation of building components (columns, beams, bracing) even if partially obscured
- Human figures at entries show the scale of the clear-span volume above
- Vehicle/parking context shows the footprint extent
- Shadows cast by the form reveal the depth and height

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Panel lines should NOT be random or irregular; they follow a precise grid pattern
- Fastener lines must be visible but not dominate; they reinforce the panel grid, not replace it
- Avoid treating the roof as smooth or seamless; standing seam creates visual rhythm
- Do NOT show architectural ornamentation or curves that contradict the industrial aesthetic
- Avoid heavy shadows that obscure the panel geometry

**Example Prompt Fragments:**
- "precise horizontal corrugated metal panels with clearly visible fastener lines every 12 inches"
- "standing seam metal roof with prominent parallel ridges and seam lines creating rhythm"
- "light gray galvanized steel walls with crisp panel lines and consistent ribbing pattern"
- "large clear span interior volume evident in the form, industrial yet functional"
- "gable roof with clean metal edges and predictable structural expression"

## Healthcare / Senior Care

**Key Visual Characteristics:**
- Residential scale and proportions (even if larger footprint, divided into smaller massing sections)
- Warm, inviting materials and color palette (earth tones, warm neutrals, natural materials)
- Accessible features shown naturally and prominently: ramps, handrails, wide entries, level transitions
- Covered drop-off or portico at main entry for weather protection and dignity in access
- Large, visible windows conveying transparency and connection to outdoors
- Welcoming entry design with clear wayfinding (not institutional or forbidding)
- Soft landscaping that appears mature and established (not stark new construction)
- Materials express care and quality, not institutional economy

**Material Tendencies:**
- Wall finishes: warm brick or stone veneer, fiber cement board, warm-toned EIFS, natural wood accents
- Roofing: pitched rooflines or varied massing heights, architectural shingles or standing seam in neutral tones
- Entry: clear glazing showing interior, welcoming color, possibly canopy or overhang
- Trim: natural wood, warm metals, accessible design details (visible handrails in coordinating colors)
- Landscape: mature trees, flowering shrubs, accessible pathways

**Scale Cues:**
- Human figures on porches, at entries, in outdoor gathering spaces
- Vehicles parked normally (not looming); appropriate parking lot scale for facility type
- Views from ground level showing pedestrian perspective, not aerial dominance
- Windows sized for human occupation, not industrial-scale facades

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Do NOT make it look institutional, hospital-like, or institutional gray
- Avoid overly modern or glass-heavy aesthetic; residential warmth is essential
- Do NOT minimize accessibility features; handrails, ramps, and curb cuts should be visible and attractive
- Avoid harsh institutional signage; wayfinding should be subtle and integrated
- Do NOT show the building as remote or unwelcoming; contextualize within community

**Example Prompt Fragments:**
- "warm brick and stone veneer with earth tones, residential scale despite larger footprint"
- "welcoming covered portico entry with generous glazing and accessible ramp clearly visible"
- "pitched roofline with architectural detailing, warm color palette suggesting care and quality"
- "mature landscaping with flowering shrubs and shade trees, established and inviting"
- "visible handrails and accessible design features integrated into warm, residential aesthetic"

## Commercial / Retail

**Key Visual Characteristics:**
- Strong storefront presence with transparent glazing showing interior activity and merchandise
- Brand expression through entry design, signage areas, and color/material choices
- Street presence and pedestrian engagement: wide transparent windows, welcoming entry, display areas
- Clear distinction between ground-floor retail activation and upper floors (if applicable)
- Visible entryways inviting customers in; not fortress-like or blank walls
- Signage areas clearly delineated and designed as part of the composition
- Material expression that suggests durability and quality appropriate to the brand
- Parking integrated naturally (not dominating) with pedestrian pathways visible

**Material Tendencies:**
- Storefronts: anodized aluminum frames with clear or lightly tinted glass, frameless glass options
- Walls: brick, fiber cement, metal panels, stone, or high-quality EIFS with visual interest
- Ground floor: higher-quality finishes, accent colors, polished or natural stone if appropriate
- Signage: integrated into facade design, not generic or cheap-looking
- Entry doors: glass and metal, clearly functional and welcoming

**Scale Cues:**
- Human figures interacting with storefront displays and windows
- Street-level perspective showing pedestrian experience
- Vehicles parked nearby suggesting activity and access
- Lighting from storefronts at dusk showing occupancy and warmth

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Do NOT show blank walls or blank-stare storefront dead zones
- Avoid generic, cheap-looking storefronts that contradict the building's intended market tier
- Do NOT minimize the glazing area; transparency is critical to retail success
- Avoid dark, forbidding entries or concealed storefronts
- Do NOT show empty or unused-looking retail spaces; suggest interior activity

**Example Prompt Fragments:**
- "transparent storefront with prominent glass display windows, interior activity visible"
- "aluminum storefront frames with clear glazing, welcoming entry design"
- "brick facade with integrated signage areas, pedestrian-scaled proportion"
- "ground-floor retail activation with warm interior lighting visible at dusk"
- "human figures at storefronts suggesting activity and engagement"

## Industrial / Warehouse

**Key Visual Characteristics:**
- Large-scale, functional aesthetic; no pretense of residential comfort or commercial polish
- High eave lines and expansive wall planes expressing the volume and utility
- Loading docks clearly visible with appropriate turning radiuses implied
- Overhead doors (sectional or roll-up) for equipment and truck access prominently shown
- Equipment yards, outdoor storage, or material staging areas shown plainly
- Utilitarian materials without finishing: exposed block, corrugated metal, concrete, minimal trim
- Structural systems often visible (exposed beams, bracing) or clearly expressed
- Minimal landscaping; hardscape dominates (concrete pads, asphalt, gravel areas)

**Material Tendencies:**
- Wall panels: bare or painted CMU block, corrugated or ribbed metal panels, no applied finishes
- Roofing: standing seam metal, typically silver/gray; sometimes black TPO; no architectural detail
- Overhead doors: standard industrial gray or color-coded for function
- Paving: plain concrete or asphalt; oil stains and weathering acceptable
- Trim: minimal; simple metal edge details, no architectural expression

**Scale Cues:**
- Vehicles (forklifts, semi-trucks) nearby showing human scale and access logic
- Human figures dwarfed by the building, emphasizing volume and industrial purpose
- Equipment, containers, or pallets in storage yards showing the building's function
- Wide-angle compositions showing the full footprint and surrounding industrial context

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Do NOT apply residential or commercial architectural details; keep it purely functional
- Avoid excessive ornamentation, color variety, or stylistic flourishes
- Do NOT minimize the overhead doors or loading infrastructure; they are primary features
- Avoid over-landscaping or softening the industrial character
- Do NOT make it look abandoned or poorly maintained; functional cleanliness is key

**Example Prompt Fragments:**
- "large clear span industrial building with exposed corrugated metal panels"
- "multiple overhead bay doors clearly visible, loading dock infrastructure prominent"
- "bare CMU walls with minimal trim, high eave line emphasizing volume"
- "equipment yard with pallets and storage visible, utilitarian hardscape"
- "industrial aesthetic functional and straightforward, no applied finishes or softening"

## Residential (Single-Family & Multi-Family)

**Key Visual Characteristics:**
- Human scale: massing, windows, doors, and details sized for residential occupation
- Pitched roof forms expressing the traditional residential architectural language
- Natural materials (or authentic-appearing) creating warmth and approachability
- Neighborhood context: setbacks, orientation, and relationship to surrounding houses
- Porches, patios, stoops, or decks shown as primary outdoor living spaces
- Visible entry at human scale with steps, landings, or threshold design
- Residential landscaping: foundation plantings, shade trees, lawn areas, fencing
- Window patterns and proportions typical of residential use (not ribbon windows or vast glass)

**Material Tendencies:**
- Siding: wood shingles, fiber cement board, brick, or stone veneer (warm tones)
- Roofing: architectural shingles, slate, or metal in traditional profiles; pitched forms
- Trim: wood or fiber cement, warm colors, traditional proportions
- Entry: wood or composite door, surrounded by trim and landing, possibly sidelights
- Windows: traditional double-hung or casement, wood or composite frames, smaller panes
- Foundation: brick, stone, or trim details at grade, not continuous concrete exposure

**Scale Cues:**
- Human figures on porches or stoops, moving through front yards
- Vehicles parked in driveway showing setback and property relationship
- Adjacent houses visible in context, showing block and neighborhood character
- Tree canopy at mature scale, providing shade and screening

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Do NOT apply commercial or institutional scale to residential design
- Avoid minimalist modern aesthetic if the context is traditional neighborhood
- Do NOT show institutional materials (CMU, corrugated metal) in residential zones
- Avoid blank front facades; residential entries must be visible and welcoming
- Do NOT minimize outdoor living features; they are central to residential appeal

**Example Prompt Fragments:**
- "pitched gable roof with architectural shingles, warm earth tones"
- "brick veneer with fiber cement trim, residential scale proportion"
- "front porch with columns and railing, welcoming entry at human scale"
- "mature shade trees and foundation plantings, established neighborhood character"
- "traditional window proportions with warm wood trim, neighborhood context visible"

## Multi-Story (2+ Stories)

**Key Visual Characteristics:**
- Vertical composition clearly expressing multiple floor levels
- Floor articulation visible through horizontal lines, balconies, or setbacks breaking the mass
- Curtain wall or punched window pattern consistent and logical across facades
- Base/middle/top expression: stronger design at grade level, lighter at top, or vice versa
- Lobby entrance clearly prominent and differentiated at ground level
- Variation in massing or upper-floor setbacks preventing a massive monolithic appearance
- Balconies or terraces shown if included, with scale indicators (human figures visible)
- Proportional relationship of glass to solid creating visual rhythm

**Material Tendencies:**
- Base: heavier materials (stone, brick, metal, darker tones) anchoring the composition
- Curtain wall: repeated mullion pattern expressing the floor structure
- Upper floors: lighter colors, possible contrast to base, consistent material expression
- Columns/piers: expressed vertically, emphasizing height and articulation
- Entry/lobby: more ornamental, architectural glass, clearly differentiated

**Scale Cues:**
- Human figures at entries and on balconies (if visible) showing occupied scale
- Ground-floor glazing showing retail, lobby, or public spaces with interior activity
- Vehicle parking nearby contextualizing the building footprint
- Aerial or elevated perspective often appropriate to show massing and form
- Surrounding buildings shown to demonstrate height relationship and context

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Do NOT create a monolithic blank facade lacking floor articulation
- Avoid random or inconsistent window patterns that suggest poor planning
- Do NOT make ground floors appear inaccessible or unmarked; entry clarity is critical
- Avoid top-heavy proportions; balance the composition vertically
- Do NOT eliminate mullion patterns that express the structural logic; they ground the design

**Example Prompt Fragments:**
- "6-story building with clear floor articulation expressed through horizontal lines"
- "curtain wall with consistent mullion pattern creating rhythm across facade"
- "heavier material base transitioning to lighter upper floors, balanced proportion"
- "prominent ground-floor lobby entrance with enhanced glazing and architectural detail"
- "setback upper floors preventing monolithic appearance, vertical articulation clear"

## Renovation / Addition

**Key Visual Characteristics:**
- Original/existing building clearly distinguished from new addition (color, material, tone)
- Material transition thoughtfully designed and visible (not just butted together)
- Context of existing building preserved and respected in the composition
- New work shown as a response to or complement of the original
- Phasing or before/after composition sometimes effective (showing the before state faintly or alongside)
- Existing features (original doors, windows, distinctive details) preserved if possible and shown
- Proportion and scale of addition relate logically to existing building

**Material Tendencies:**
- Existing: original materials, patina, and character maintained (not over-cleaned or modernized)
- New: may contrast or complement depending on design intent; clearly new but respectful
- Transition: compatible materials, thoughtful joint details, harmonious color relationships
- Trim: detailing at the junction clearly designed and appropriate

**Scale Cues:**
- Composition shows both existing and new building; one does not dominate inappropriately
- Human figures show scale relationship between old and new
- Shadows or time-of-day lighting may suggest the two phases (not necessary but effective)

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Do NOT make the addition appear to be a design accident or poor retrofit
- Avoid destroying the character of the original building for the new work
- Do NOT show the existing building over-cleaned or modernized if the design intent is preservation
- Avoid jarring material clashes without thoughtful transition design
- Do NOT obscure or minimize the existing building's presence in the composition

**Example Prompt Fragments:**
- "original brick building preserved with new contemporary addition in complementary materials"
- "existing character maintained in fore, new aluminum and glass addition responds to original proportions"
- "material transition thoughtfully detailed, new limestone base connects to original foundation"
- "before and after composition suggesting the renovation's relationship to existing structure"
- "original wood windows preserved, new glazing systems reference historic proportions"

## Educational (K-12, Higher Ed)

**Key Visual Characteristics:**
- Campus context visible: multiple buildings, open spaces, athletic fields, or quad areas
- Multiple distinct entries at different points (main entry prominent, but secondary entries clear)
- Covered walkways or colonnade connecting buildings, suggesting flow and connection
- Play areas (K-12) or athletic fields visible in composition, showing youth activity
- Institutional scale but with welcoming character: not monumental but purposeful
- Flagpole, outdoor gathering spaces, or plaza areas suggesting community gathering
- Mix of academic structures (classrooms, labs, admin) expressed in massing variety
- Proportions and materials appropriate to education mission: durable, high-quality, inspiring

**Material Tendencies:**
- Primary walls: brick, stone, or high-quality EIFS in warm institutional tones (tan, warm gray)
- Roofing: pitched or modern sloped forms; quality materials reflecting durability
- Entry vestibules: glass and wood, generous proportions, welcoming design
- Trim: coordinated colors, quality wood or composite materials
- Glazing: substantial windows at occupied spaces, translucent or frosted at service areas

**Scale Cues:**
- Campus plan or multiple buildings visible in single composition (plan view or elevated perspective)
- Human figures (children, students, staff) interacting with spaces, showing appropriate scale
- Athletic fields or play equipment visible if part of the campus
- Vehicles (buses, cars) at parking areas or drop-off zones showing transportation context

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Do NOT make it look sterile, cold, or purely institutional without warmth
- Avoid minimizing the outdoor learning and gathering spaces
- Do NOT show campus as disconnected buildings; connectivity and flow are essential
- Avoid blank walls facing common areas; transparency and visual connection matter
- Do NOT eliminate human activity; campuses should suggest vibrant educational use

**Example Prompt Fragments:**
- "warm brick and stone academic buildings arranged around central quad"
- "covered walkway connecting multiple structures, campus flow and connectivity visible"
- "generous main entry with glass vestibule, welcoming institutional scale"
- "athletic field and outdoor gathering spaces visible in campus context"
- "student and staff figures moving between buildings, vibrant educational activity"

## Religious / Assembly (Churches, Temples, Mosques, Event Venues)

**Key Visual Characteristics:**
- Prominent, dignified entry or gathering space clearly expressing the building's sacred or assembly purpose
- Height and volume emphasized to express gathering significance or spiritual aspiration
- Primary gathering space (sanctuary, prayer hall, event center) massing expressed distinctly
- Secondary spaces (entry vestibule, offices, support areas) subordinate to primary function
- Parking and gathering areas shown, suggesting congregation or event congregation
- Material expression suggesting permanence, quality, and respect for the function
- Symbolic elements (bell tower, steeple, dome, crescent moon, prominent portico) if applicable
- Composition often benefits from elevation or slightly dramatic perspective expressing significance

**Material Tendencies:**
- Primary walls: stone, quality brick, or refined EIFS; often lighter or warmer tones suggesting permanence
- Roofing: pitched rooflines with distinct profiles, quality materials; sometimes domes or soaring forms
- Entry: prominent portico, grand doors, generous glazing showing interior light/activity
- Interior glow at dusk: warm light visible through glazing from sanctuary or gathering space
- Trim and details: ornamental, crafted appearance; not utilitarian
- Parking and gathering: generous, open, welcoming proportions

**Scale Cues:**
- Human figures at entry and gathering areas emphasize the building's scale
- Vehicles parked showing community gathering and transportation access
- Evening/dusk lighting often emphasizes interior warm glow and sacred character
- Neighboring buildings or context showing the building's prominence in the community

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Do NOT minimize or obscure the primary function; gathering and spiritual significance must be evident
- Avoid utilitarian or industrial aesthetic; reverence and permanence are essential
- Do NOT make the entry appear small or secondary; primary entry should be dignified and visible
- Avoid harsh, cold lighting; warm and spiritual atmosphere matters
- Do NOT obscure parking or gathering areas needed for the assembly function

**Example Prompt Fragments:**
- "prominent portico entry with tall glazed doors, interior warm light visible"
- "soaring gable roof expressing sacred volume and gathering significance"
- "stone and brick exterior with crafted detail, permanent and dignified expression"
- "warm interior glow at dusk visible through sanctuary glazing, spiritual atmosphere"
- "generous parking area and gathering spaces visible, community gathering capacity clear"

## Summary: Prompt Engineering Best Practices by Type

1. **Research the type first**: Understand the specific functional and aesthetic requirements before constructing the prompt.
2. **Emphasize distinctive features**: Each type has signature elements; make them prominent and clearly rendered.
3. **Balance function and form**: Show how the design serves its purpose while expressing architectural intent.
4. **Include human scale**: Use figures, vehicles, or activity to ground the rendering in reality.
5. **Context matters**: Show how the building relates to its setting and community.
6. **Avoid type-specific clich√©s**: Don't resort to stereotypes; use descriptive, specific language instead.
7. **Test and iterate**: If a rendering misses the type's character, refine the prompt with more specific material or compositional language.
