# Back-Charge Procedures Reference

## Overview

A back-charge occurs when a subcontractor fails to perform contractually required work and the GC — or another party arranged by the GC — performs the work and charges the cost back to the responsible sub. Back-charges are among the most contentious disputes in construction. Proper, contemporaneous documentation is the difference between a recoverable cost and an unenforceable claim.

The goal is always to give the subcontractor every reasonable opportunity to self-correct. Back-charging is a last resort, not a first response.

---

## Back-Charge Trigger Conditions

A back-charge is justified only when ALL of the following conditions are satisfied:

1. The work is clearly within the subcontractor's contracted scope of work
2. The deficiency was documented in writing and the sub was formally notified
3. The sub was given a reasonable, specific deadline to correct the deficiency (per subcontract terms — typically 48–72 hours for punch items)
4. The sub failed to complete the correction within the stated deadline
5. The GC performed the correction using their own forces, another subcontractor, or a third-party vendor, and has receipts to document actual costs

If any of these conditions is not met, the back-charge may not be enforceable. Do not proceed without satisfying all five.

## Required Documentation

Maintain the following documentation for every back-charge event. Assume every back-charge will be contested and document accordingly.

### 1. Initial Notice to Subcontractor

- Written notice (email is acceptable; platform message with delivery confirmation is preferred)
- Identifies the deficiency by punch item ID and written description
- References the specific subcontract clause requiring the work
- States the deadline for correction in explicit terms (date and time, not "within 48 hours")
- States explicitly that failure to correct by the deadline will result in a back-charge to the sub's account
- Proof of delivery: email read receipt, signed acknowledgment, or certified mail confirmation

### 2. Evidence of the Deficiency

- Before photos per photo-tagging-guide.md standards (close-up + wide shot, annotated)
- Written reference to the specification section, drawing detail, or subcontract clause that was not met
- Inspection report or inspection note if a formal inspection produced the deficiency record
- Punch item ID linking the back-charge to the original punch list record

### 3. Evidence of the Correction

- After photos confirming the deficiency was resolved (taken by GC or third party performing the work)
- Daily report or field note recording the date and personnel who performed the corrective work
- Labor records: names, hours worked, hourly billing rates
- Material receipts: itemized invoice or receipt showing materials purchased, quantities, and unit costs
- Equipment costs with rate documentation if equipment was required
- Third-party invoices if an outside vendor was engaged

### 4. Cost Summary

| Cost Category | Description | Hours / Qty | Rate | Subtotal |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|------|----------|
| Labor | Foreman, journeymen, laborers | — | $/hr | — |
| Materials | Itemized by type | — | $/unit | — |
| Equipment | Lifts, tools, etc. | — | $/day | — |
| Third-party | Specialty vendor invoice | — | Lump sum | — |
| Supervision | Superintendent time to manage | — | $/hr | — |
| OH&P markup | Per subcontract (typically 10–15%) | — | % | — |
| **Total** | | | | — |

The OH&P markup is only applicable if the subcontract explicitly permits it. Verify before applying.

## Subcontract Requirements

Every back-charge must be administered in strict accordance with the subcontract terms. Review the following provisions in the applicable subcontract before issuing any back-charge notice:

| Provision | Typical Terms | Where to Find |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Notice period | 48–72 hours written notice before performing work (some contracts: 5–7 business days) | Article on Default or Back-Charge |
| Cure period | Additional time after notice before GC may proceed | Same article |
| Markup allowed | OH&P on corrective work costs (typically 10–15%) | Same article or General Conditions |
| Dispute resolution | Mediation, arbitration, or direct negotiation | Dispute Resolution article |
| Payment method | Deducted from next pay application or via separate invoice | Payment article |

Never apply a back-charge without confirming the notice and cure periods. A back-charge executed before the cure period expires is procedurally defective and may be unenforceable.

## Back-Charge Process Timeline

```
Item identified (punch list)
        ↓
Written notice issued to sub — deadline stated
        ↓
Deadline passes — sub has not corrected
        ↓
Back-charge intent notice issued (if subcontract requires second notice)
        ↓
GC performs corrective work — all costs documented in real time
        ↓
Back-charge letter issued to sub with cost summary and documentation package
        ↓
Sub responds: accepts or disputes
        ↓
    [Accepted] → Deduct from next pay application
    [Disputed] → Escalate per subcontract dispute resolution process
```

## Integration with Other Processes

- **Punch list**: Back-charges originate from punch items. Always link the back-charge record to the original `PUNCH-{NNN}` ID for a complete audit trail.
- **Pay applications**: Approved back-charges appear as deductions on the subcontractor's next Schedule of Values pay application. Document the deduction line clearly with the back-charge reference number.
- **Change order tracker**: If the back-charge is significant in value or contested, document it as a deductive change order per contract requirements. Some prime contracts require owner notification for back-charges above a threshold.
- **Claims documentation**: If the sub formally disputes the back-charge, escalate to the claims-documentation workflow. The back-charge documentation package becomes the evidentiary foundation for the claim.

## Common Back-Charge Categories

| Category | Typical Scenario | Typical Cost Range |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Incomplete punch items | Paint touch-up or caulking not done by deadline | $200 – $2,000 |
| Damage to other trades | Electrician damages finished drywall or flooring | $500 – $5,000 |
| Clean-up failure | Sub leaves debris; GC arranges cleanup | $300 – $3,000 |
| Protection failure | Sub fails to protect finished floors or millwork | $1,000 – $10,000 |
| Warranty callback | Sub unresponsive to warranty deficiency call-back | $500 – $5,000 |
| Material substitution | Sub installs non-specified material without approval | Varies widely |

## Best Practices

- **Give the sub every fair opportunity to self-correct.** A phone call before the formal written notice often resolves items faster and preserves the working relationship.
- **Document in real time.** Reconstruct documentation after the fact is legally weak and practically difficult. Record labor hours, material purchases, and photos on the same day the corrective work occurs.
- **Follow the subcontract exactly.** Notice periods and cure periods are not suggestions. A procedurally defective back-charge is the GC's liability, not the sub's.
- **Keep amounts reasonable and fully supported.** Inflated or unsupported back-charges invite disputes and damage credibility for all future interactions with that sub.
- **Maintain a professional tone in all communications.** Back-charges are a contractual business mechanism, not a punitive measure. Avoid accusatory language in notices.
- **Exercise judgment on small items.** Pursuing a $150 back-charge against a sub who has otherwise performed well and has significant remaining contract value may cost more in relationship damage than it recovers. Evaluate each situation in context.
- **Notify the sub immediately when corrective work is complete.** Do not wait until the pay application cycle to inform them of the charge. Early notification reduces surprise and dispute frequency.
